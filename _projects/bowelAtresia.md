This project began through the UBC Engineers in Scrubs program, which exposed me to various clinical domains and guided me in applying a needs-based approach to medical technology innovation. Our interdisciplinary team of four engineering students and surgical residents collaborated with the UBC Global Surgery Lab. Under the supervision of Dr. Shahrzad Joharifard, we developed a low-cost high-fidelity simulator to support training in the treatment of neonatal bowel atresia. This project aimed to improve training opportunities in resource constrained settings with limited access to pediatric surgical expertise.

I drove the engineering design process from concept to validation, iterating on the bowel atresia model with surgeon feedback to balance anatomical realism, manufacturability, and cost. The final system reduced cost to one-third of existing alternatives while maintaining high fidelity, as demonstrated through evaluation with 12 surgeons showing strong construct validity. I additionally received the SBME Trainee Research Award to fund this project. Our work has been [featured by SBME]( https://bme.ubc.ca/sbme-engineers-in-scrubs-team-helps-surgeons-treating-newborns-with-small-bowel-blockages/) and is being used to support surgical training in South Sudan in collaboration with Médecins Sans Frontières.
